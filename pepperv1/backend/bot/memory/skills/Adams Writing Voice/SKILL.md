After a car accident, Simon Jarret experienced brain hemorrhaging that would slowly lead to his death; however, with a promising new technology, a non-invasive scan of a brain could be made that was so detailed it could be used to save him. This scan would also make a copy of every memory, setting the scene for the 2015 horror game, SOMA. After making his way to a Doctor's Office in Toronto, you get into a machine similar to the ones used to take X-ray images at dental offices. As Simon gets out of the machine, you find yourself at the bottom of the ocean, in a world that has been ravaged by a comet that left the surface uninhabitable, in a decrepit research facility run by an AI who took the order to preserve mankind as to copy consciousnesses and upload them into robots that think they are human. The copy of Simon played by you has been used as a default copy to test the transfer of human consciousness for decades. The game's finale sees you get into a similar machine, your brain being copied, as a hard drive with every copied human consciousness in a digital paradise is shot into space - only this time, when the scan is done, the main character is still at the bottom of the ocean. "We were the unlucky ones” - those who didn’t wake up on the other side, those who woke in a nightmare instead of living a full life, cured of brain hemorrhaging.
What has stuck with me so much is the morals of a world in which our consciousness isn't continuous. Further, in the game Wolfenstein II, one of the main characters refuses to sleep, because when we lose consciousness, our brains stop, someone wakes up with the same memories and experiences, but is it still us? While these questions often deal with ideas of the self, I want to interrogate what this means for how we should live.
I feel it's important to first justify why this is important. Often, when making decisions, especially philosophical ones, we consider the entirety of our lives and what does it matter if we have short instances where we are not thinking? Considering this perspective has value because when discussing how we should live, one might want to first consider how do we live, and we do so, seemingly with "no ability to have acted differently in the past"(Sam Harris) and with only the continuous experience of each instance, as if we had gotten control of this body for a day, as Marcus Aurelius in Mediations Book 3 "Each of us lives only now, this brief instant. The rest has been lived already, or is impossible to see." While this might be logically a step, consider that you had been abducted and replaced in your sleep with a copy last night, and that this would be indistinguishable from sleep. Further, I will make the case for the utility in thinking like this.
In regard to the ethics of living with the lifespan of a day, I will begin by addressing the responsibility to a life we hold for brief periods of time, then considering my personal experience of waking up each day being the "unlucky one," the one working for a tomorrow I don't see. Similar to determinism, on its face, this might seem like a restricting way to view life, but on the contrary, I think there is value in considering this outlook, that even a person who might believe in free will can gain compassion from occasionally giving some credit to a causal universe.
If we accept this idea, that due to lapse in consciousness, we are essentially the caretakers of a life we did not live, then what should we do with this body, and what do we owe our future selves and all the people who will soon be nothing as our shift ends? This situation on its face can make utilitarianism much easier to understand, that our actions "are right in proportion as it tend to promote happiness, wrong as they tend to produce the reverse of happiness. (JS Mill) " . A consequentialist might conclude that our days should be filled with long working hours, only to then volunteer late into the night, we can conceive of picking what actions could lead to the most good for our community. Similar to the ideas of the lecture The Afterlife by Scheffler, it is the world continuing on which gives us meaning, that “the coming into existence of people we do not know or love matters more to us than our own survival and the survival of those we know and love.”(45) But on its face, this seems like a poor way to live, as though you were endlessly giving your life to a future you that will never reap the rewards. So maybe we could live a life like Omelas, idealistic due to the suffering of one, part of our life a torturous amount of work, building relationships, and strong foundations, so our future selves may live a dream. But, like those who left Omelas, I do see another way. 
As a sophomore in high school, I first took an interest in philosophy, not only as a means to deal with concerns about my lack of control and distant death, but also as motivation to explore life, pursue knowledge, and to attempt to be something great. I often thought of myself as three people: Past Adam, Future Adam, and Present Adam, and I would tell myself that I would live each day for my future self, to work and learn so that he would be better. It was comforting to feel cared for by my past self, who worked for something they would not experience, and in the moment, it was that I would work for the sake of my future self and the people my future self could help. More than this, it meant that day to day, it was my job to intellectually enrich myself to experience joy and what JS Mill might call higher pleasures. Since I lived in the present, working for tomorrow, it could best be described as a form of virtue ethics, of self-actualization, so I could meet the needs of the future. Yet, there won’t always be a tomorrow, what matters to us now might mean nothing to our future selves, the people we help will eventually die, so what do we gain from viewing life through this lens? It is as though by viewing our entire lives as mostly repetitive days that it becomes less bearable, that instead of continually rolling up a boulder only for it to fall down, we awake into the world halfway up a mountain with a choice to give up or to climb higher despite our short existence. Unlike Sisyphus standing at the foot of a mountain, we stand with the burden of our past; we could neglect it, we could have our day off, or we could choose to continue rolling it up, continuing to strive for something, “the struggle itself could fill a man’s heart.”(10) 

When living each day as though it is only one part in a story that you will not finish, one can find newfound compassion not only for oneself but for others. The angry cashier and the person who cut you off have awoken to find themselves with memories and trauma to overcome that they did not choose; they had no control over their past selves and are working with the knowledge given to them. Beyond this, living with such a short time span, allows us to consider how we want to spend our lives, how much more beautiful the sunset is when it is the last thing we will ever see, and to know that it will be a memory for thousands of lives after you. Beyond this, so many philosophers have claimed that it is good to see with lucidity. Mencius believed it took cultivating our moral intuitions, that a “Superior Person” with eyes wants to see clearly(16:10, Confucius). We first learned about Descartes and the quest to know what we could know, that what is real are the distinct forms we can think of. “For even when painters try to create sirens and satyrs with the most extraordinary bodies… they simply jumble up the limbs of different animals.”(2, Descartes) There seems to be something undeniably valuable about the pursuit of the genuine reality, even the hedonists and utilitarians think it is inherently good and fundamental to maximize and pursue pleasure while avoiding pain. There is a sense of lucidity in this way of viewing the world; we feel we have the experience of continuity, but each moment our “spirits [are] vomited out and gulped in again each instance(Book 2, Section 2, Aurelius)” our interests, our annoyances, and motivations change constantly. The difference between a continuous and non-continuous sense of self is merely an arbitrary jump too large for us to comprehend. “The unitary self is an illusion and that we are all composed of successive selves”(49) according to Brison. When an event exceeds what narrative we can construct, this sense disappears, but sleep, a pause in consciousness, is easy to comprehend. So if one were pursuing a good life, this sense of lucidity in seeing how we are different each moment and each day gives further justification to this daily self. 
Simon Jarret woke up in a hellish world, the lives of the people before him leading to the one in which he would remain undying at the bottom on an ocean. Yet, we like Simon, have control over where we wake up, or at least where our future selves and those people we touch with our actions. Like taking for granted the fact that everyone lives after we die, it is easy to forget how convenient it can be to wake up and appreciate the works of our past selves, to blame others for the work of their deviant caretakers. So, why not thank yourself occasionally, or thank your future self by leaving them a beautiful sunrise to remember.
